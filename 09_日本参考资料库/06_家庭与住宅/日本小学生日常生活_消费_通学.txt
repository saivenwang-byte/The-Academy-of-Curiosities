## Reference Document for Children's Mystery Series Set in Nagoya

## 1. コンビニ (Convenience Stores) Culture

### The Big Three

Japanese convenience stores (コンビニ, *konbini*) are a universe unto themselves. In Nagoya, as everywhere in Japan, three chains dominate every corner:

| Chain | Color Scheme | Kids' Nickname | Nagoya Presence |
|-------|-------------|----------------|-----------------|
| **セブン-イレブン (7-Eleven)** | Orange / Green / Red | セブン (*Sebun*) | Very strong; more per square km than Tokyo in some wards |
| **ローソン (Lawson)** | Blue / White / Pink | ローソン (*Rōson*) | Strong; known for からあげクン and MACHI café |
| **ファミリーマート (FamilyMart)** | Green / Blue / White | ファミマ (*Famima*) | Ubiquitous; the iconic 入店音 is a cultural touchstone |

### What Kids Buy With おこづかい (Allowance)

The 100 yen (plus tax = 110 yen) price point is the sweet spot. Kids under 10 rarely carry bills; it's all coins jingling in a がま口 (frog-mouth coin purse) or character wallet.

**The Classics (駄菓子 — Cheap Sweets Division):**

| Item | Price | Description |
|------|-------|-------------|
| うまい棒 (*Umaibō*) | **10 yen** | Puffed corn stick; flavors include めんたい (mentaiko), コーンポタージュ (corn potage), チーズ (cheese), たこやき (takoyaki), and the legendary 納豆 (nattō). The 10-yen price has been stable for decades—the packaging just got slightly thinner. A kid with 100 yen can buy 10. |
| チロルチョコ (*Tirol Choco*) | **20–50 yen** | Tiny square chocolates in wildly varied flavors: きなこもち (kinako mochi), ミルク (milk), いちご (strawberry), and seasonal limited editions like 桜もち or 抹茶. The packaging is collectible. |
| ガリガリ君 (*Garigari-kun*) | **70–100 yen** | Iconic ice pop by 赤城乳業 (Akagi Dairy). The ソーダ (soda) flavor is the classic, but 梨 (nashi pear) is a summer hit. Famous for the "当たり" (winner) lottery—bite the stick and if you see 当たり, you get a free one. This is a major life event for a 4th grader. |
| ブラックサンダー (*Black Thunder*) | **30–50 yen** | Chocolate bar with cocoa cookie pieces. The package says "若い女性に人気" (popular with young women) but elementary boys buy it too. |
| キャベツ太郎 (*Kyabetsu Tarō*) | **30 yen** | Corn snack the size of a fist, flavored with ソース (sauce). Greasy, salty, perfect. |
| ココアシガレット (*Cocoa Cigarette*) | **30 yen** | Cocoa-flavored candy sticks in a box designed like cigarettes. Controversial with some parents, endlessly cool to kids. |
| よっちゃんイカ (*Yocchan Ika*) | **30 yen** | Vinegared squid jerky in a small yellow packet. Acquired taste; the kid who eats this has street cred. |
| ラムネ (*Ramune*) | **40–60 yen** | Carbonated candy tablets in a small bottle-shaped container. The marble-inside-the-bottle soda version is for festivals, but the candy tablets are everyday. |

**セブン-イレブン Exclusives:**
- ななチキ (*Nanachiki*) — small fried chicken chunks; 100–150 yen
- からあげ棒 — chicken on a stick; greasy fingers on the way home from school
- The セブンカフェ (Seven Café) ココア — 100 yen; kids sometimes buy this in winter to feel grown-up

**ローソン Exclusives:**
- からあげクン (*Kara-age-kun*) — bite-sized fried chicken in a distinctive red cup; flavors rotate: レギュラー (regular), レッド (spicy), チーズ, 北海道チーズ, seasonal ones like てりやき or ゆず胡椒
- MACHI café ココア — slightly more premium feel
- Exclusive ポケモン / すみっコぐらし goods during campaigns

**ファミリーマート Exclusives:**
- ファミチキ (*Famichiki*) — the flagship fried chicken; a rite of passage is buying one after school with saved-up coins
- コロッケ (croquette) in the hot case — 80–100 yen
- The 入店音 (entrance chime) — see below

### The おでん (Oden) Counter — Winter Only

From roughly October through March, a heated stainless steel vat appears near the register. It's divided into rectangular compartments, each holding a different oden item simmering in light brown dashi broth.

**Kids' Oden Hierarchy:**
1. 大根 (*daikon*) — the king; thick-cut radish soaked with broth, served in a cup with a dab of からし (Japanese mustard) on the lid. 100 yen.
2. たまご (*tamago*) — whole boiled egg, broth-infused. 80 yen.
3. ちくわ (*chikuwa*) — tube-shaped fish cake. 70 yen.
4. こんにゃく (*konnyaku*) — grey jelly block, triangular; suspicious to foreigners, normal to kids. 60 yen.
5. はんぺん (*hanpen*) — white, fluffy fish cake triangle. 60 yen.
6. 餅入り巾着 (*mochi-iri kinchaku*) — fried tofu pouch with mochi inside. The best. 120 yen. Splurge item.
7. ウィンナー (sausage) — bright red sausage. 80 yen.

The clerk fishes items out with a long pair of chopsticks, drops them into a thin plastic cup, ladles in some broth, and snaps on a lid. The heat through the cup on cold fingers is an essential winter memory.

### 肉まん (Nikuman) — Winter Counterpart

- Hot steamed buns from the same counter area
- 肉まん (pork bun), ピザまん (pizza bun — tomato sauce and cheese inside), あんまん (sweet red bean bun), カレーまん
- 100–150 yen
- Wrapped in thin wax paper that says the name in hiragana or katakana
- The ピザまん is the universal kid favorite

### くじ (Lottery) and キャンペーン (Campaigns)

Convenience stores run constant promotions that function as kid-gambling:

- **レシートくじ (Receipt Lottery)**: Spend over 500 yen (or 700 depending on chain), get a scratch-off or pull-tab card at the register. Prizes range from another おでん item to a free drink.
- **ポイントカード (Point Cards)**: Kids' parents use these, but kids learn the system early. T-point (FamilyMart), Ponta (Lawson), nanaco (7-Eleven).
- **一番くじ (Ichiban Kuji)**: Standing box displays near the register with A賞, B賞, C賞, etc. Each draw is 500–700 yen. Prizes are character goods (鬼滅の刃, ポケモン, ドラゴンボール, すみっコぐらし). The last-prize (ラストワン賞) is often a figure that goes for a premium on Mercari. A kid might save allowance for weeks to try 2–3 draws.
- **シールキャンペーン (Sticker Campaigns)**: Buy specific items, get a sticker. Collect X stickers, get a free plate/bowl/towel. Kids participate via their mothers.
- **ポケモンキャンペーン**: 7-Eleven does Pokémon tie-ins where buying certain snacks gets you a limited Pokémon sticker or card. This drives kids into a frenzy.

### The Iconic Entrance Sound

Every convenience store chain has a distinctive electronic door chime that plays when the automatic door slides open:

- **ファミリーマート (FamilyMart)**: The 入店音 (*nyūten'on*) —「ファミマ♪ファミマ♪ファミリーン♪マートー♪」— is so famous it has become a cultural meme. There was a minor controversy in the 2010s when the Melodian Society claimed it was copied from something; Famima changed it briefly, public outcry, changed it back.
- **7-Eleven**: "チャラララッ チャラララッ♪ チャラッ チャララララッ♪"
- **Lawson**: "ポロンポローン♪" — a softer, more elegant piano chime (matches their slightly more sophisticated positioning)

Kids can identify each store by its door chime with their eyes closed. It's a common game. A Nagoya kid walking home from school will hear: Famima at the corner → Sebun two blocks later → Rōson across from the park.

### コンビニ as Social Hub

For elementary schoolers, the konbini functions as:
- **待ち合わせ場所 (Meeting spot)**: "じゃあ、ローソン角で3時にね" (Meet at the Lawson on the corner at 3pm)
- **雨宿り (Rain shelter)**: If caught in sudden rain, duck into the nearest konbini
- **トイレ (Toilet)**: Clean, free, and available; konbini are informal public restrooms
- **おつかい (Errand completion point)**: Mom sends kid with a note: "牛乳と食パン買ってきて"
- **コピー機 (Copy machine)**: Multi-function machines that print, copy, fax, and print photos from smartphones
- **宅配便 (Package drop-off/pickup)**: Kids see parents sending and receiving packages here
- **チケット発券 (Ticket issuing)**: Concert tickets, event tickets, ディズニー tickets — a konbini machine

## 2. 給食 (School Lunch) — Variations and Kids' Reactions

### System Overview

Japanese school lunch (給食, *kyūshoku*) is served in the classroom, not a cafeteria. Students take turns as 給食当番 (*kyūshoku tōban*, lunch duty) — wearing white masks, white caps, and white aprons — to serve their classmates. The meal is eaten at desks pushed together in groups (班, *han*).

Post-COVID, many schools shifted to eating facing forward in rows (黙食 *mokushoku* — silent eating), which was deeply unpopular. By 2023–2024, most schools had returned to group eating.

In Nagoya specifically, school lunches are managed by the 名古屋市教育委員会 (Nagoya City Board of Education), with 326 municipal elementary schools serving centrally planned menus.

### Most Loved 給食 Menus

**Tier 1 — Universal Consensus Favorites:**

1. **カレーライス (Curry Rice)**: The undisputed champion. Japanese school curry is mildly sweet, thickened with flour and butter (roux-based), filled with potatoes, carrots, onions, and small meat pieces. Served with 麦ごはん (barley rice — healthier, slightly chewier than pure white rice). The class falls silent when curry day arrives except for the clatter of spoons. The smell of curry in the hallway at 11:30 AM is anticipatory torture.

2. **揚げパン (Age-pan — Fried Bread)**: A コッペパン (koppe-pan, elongated soft bread roll) that has been deep-fried and rolled in きなこ (roasted soybean powder) mixed with sugar, or sometimes ココア (cocoa powder) and sugar. Golden-brown, slightly crispy outside, pillowy inside. The surface dusts fingers and faces with sweet powder. *This is the single most nostalgia-loaded school lunch item in Japan.* In Nagoya, the きなこ version is standard; ココア is a special variant. A survey of Japanese adults consistently ranks 揚げパン as the #1 "給食で好きだったメニュー" (favorite school lunch menu).

3. **ソフト麺 (Soft Noodles)**: A unique school lunch item — a sealed plastic bag containing soft, pre-cooked wheat noodles. Kids tear open the bag and dump the noodles onto their plate, then ladle ミートソース (meat sauce) or あんかけ (starch-thickened sauce) over them. The ritual of opening the bag (using teeth when scissors aren't available) is part of the experience. Messy. Glorious.

4. **冷凍みかん (Frozen Mikan)**: A whole satsuma mandarin orange, frozen solid. Served in summer. The peel is rock-hard; the flesh inside is like sorbet. Eating technique varies: some kids peel it slowly, others gnaw at it like animals. A frozen mikan on a hot June day is a small miracle.

5. **わかめご飯 (Wakame Rice)**: Plain white rice mixed with salted wakame seaweed and sesame seeds. Simple but adored. Sometimes topped with ちりめんじゃこ (tiny dried sardines).

6. **くじらの竜田揚げ (Whale Tatsuta-age)**: Deep-fried whale meat — a rapidly disappearing tradition. Whale was a staple of postwar school lunches because it was cheap protein; now it's a rare specialty item. Kids who get this in Nagoya schools today are experiencing living history. The meat is dark, slightly chewy, breaded and fried. Reactions range from "すごくおいしい" to "…これは何?".

7. **クリスマスデザート (Christmas Dessert)**: On the 給食 day closest to December 25, a special dessert appears — a small Christmas cake (square of sponge with cream and a Santa chocolate), chocolate pudding in a Christmas cup, or いちごゼリー with a star-shaped nata de coco piece. This is a core memory for most Japanese children.

**Tier 2 — Generally Liked:**
- 焼きそば (fried noodles)
- ハンバーグ (hamburg steak, in デミグラスソース)
- さばの味噌煮 (miso-simmered mackerel)
- 白身魚のフライ (fried white fish with tartar sauce)
- すまし汁 (clear soup) with とうふ and わかめ
- みそ汁 — especially 豚汁 (tonjiru, pork miso soup) in winter
- フルーツポンチ (fruit punch — mixed fruit in clear jelly/syrup)
- プリン (custard pudding — the little plastic cup variety)

### Disliked Items — The Hierarchy of Dread

1. **レバー (Liver)**: Universally the most dreaded item. Usually レバニラ炒め (liver and garlic chive stir-fry) or レバーの甘辛煮 (sweet-savory simmered liver). The metallic taste, the grainy texture, and the distinct smell make it the #1 item kids try to hide or pass to friends. A standard drama: the teacher insisting "レバーには鉄分がたくさんあるから、一口だけ食べてみよう" (Liver has lots of iron, just try one bite), and the kid holding it in their mouth, unable to swallow, eventually excusing themselves to the bathroom.

2. **セロリ (Celery)**: The stringy texture and strong smell. Often appears in スープ or as サラダ. Kids will meticulously pick out every visible piece before eating.

3. **ピーマン (Green Pepper)**: Bitter. Often appears in チンジャオロース (pepper steak stir-fry). The classic "ピーマン嫌い" (I hate green pepper) is a trope so common it appears in anime, manga, and educational comics.

4. **ゴーヤ (Bitter Melon)**: Appears in Nagoya-adjacent menus occasionally (Aichi grows a fair amount of vegetables). The bitterness is extreme; even many adults dislike it.

5. **トマト (Raw Tomato)**: Cherry tomatoes in salad. The sudden burst of juice and the skin texture divide kids sharply.

6. **豆 (Beans)**: Especially 大豆 (soybeans) in various preparations. Boiled beans, beans in salad, beans in curry (the horror). "豆嫌い" is a recognized category.

7. **牛乳 (Milk)**: Not the milk itself — the *requirement* to drink one small carton (200ml) daily. Every kid must drink it. Lactose-intolerant kids (more common in Japan than in the West) suffer. The 牛乳パック (milk carton) must be flattened and washed for recycling — part of the post-lunch cleanup ritual. In Nagoya schools, the 牛乳 is typically from 愛知県産 (Aichi Prefecture), delivered in blue-and-white 200ml cartons.

8. **ひじき (Hijiki Seaweed)**: Black, stringy, served in sweet-savory simmered dishes. Nutritious but visually off-putting to kids.

### 給食の思い出 (Memorable School Lunch Moments)

- **おかわりじゃんけん (Seconds Rock-Paper-Scissors)**: When there's leftover food (always the popular items), the teacher announces おかわり available. Kids who want it gather and play じゃんけん (rock-paper-scissors) in a tournament format. The winner gets the extra 揚げパン or the last piece of 唐揚げ. This is high-stakes drama. Accusations of あいこ (tie) cheating are common. The kid who wins the 揚げパンおかわりじゃんけん is a temporary class hero.

- **給食センター事故 (Lunch Center Incidents)**: Occasionally, a foreign object is found in the food — a piece of plastic, a hair, a small insect. The entire class stops eating. The teacher must call the 給食センター (central kitchen). Legendary stories circulate: "去年、隣のクラスの牛乳にゴキブリが…" (Last year, a cockroach in the next class's milk…)

- **The Overflowing Plate**: A kid who serves too generously, leaving insufficient portions for the last students in line. The teacher's quiet intervention. The guilty server's shame.

- **校長先生の検食 (Principal's Taste Check)**: The principal eats the school lunch 30 minutes before students do, to check for safety and quality. Kids know this as 検食 (kenshoku). If the principal gets sick, lunch is cancelled.

- **アレルギー対応 (Allergy Accommodations)**: Kids with egg, dairy, wheat, or nut allergies eat separate 除去食 (removal meals) or bring 弁当. They sit slightly apart, or at the same table with a visual barrier. This social separation is a significant part of the allergic kid's school experience.

### Nagoya-Specific School Lunch Notes

Nagoya's school lunch system has local peculiarities:

- **味噌カツ (Miso Katsu)** appears occasionally — a Nagoya specialty, modified for school lunch: bite-sized pork cutlets with red miso sauce
- **きしめん (Kishimen)** — flat udon noodles, a Nagoya specialty, served in light broth
- **てんむす (Tenmusu)** — rice balls with tempura shrimp inside, a Nagoya-origin food
- **ういろう (Uirō)** sometimes appears as dessert — a steamed rice cake unique to Nagoya; kids either love it or find it weird
- **赤だし (Akadashi)** — dark red miso soup using 八丁味噌 (Hatchō miso), a Nagoya staple; much stronger and saltier than the 白味噌 common in Kyoto/Osaka

## 3. Japanese Home Food for Kids

### おやつ (After-School Snacks)

The 3 PM snack is almost religious. Kids come home, dump the ランドセル (randoseru, the hard leather backpack), and demand something to eat.

**Nagoya-Specific Snack Culture:**
- **小倉トースト (Ogura Toast)** — thick toast with butter and あんこ (sweet red bean paste), served at 喫茶店 (kissaten, coffee shops). Kids are introduced to this by grandparents visiting the local モーニング (morning set — buy a coffee, get free toast/egg). The コメダ珈琲店 (Komeda Coffee, founded in Nagoya) experience is part of Nagoya childhood.
- **手羽先 (Tebasaki)** — Nagoya-style chicken wings: fried without batter, coated in sweet-spicy sauce with sesame seeds and pepper. Not a kid snack per se — more like what dad brings home from 世界の山ちゃん (Yamachan) or 風来坊 (Fūraibō) for a special dinner.
- **味噌煮込みうどん (Miso Nikomi Udon)** — thick noodles in red miso broth, cooked in a clay pot. Winter comfort food. Kids complain it's too hot (the clay pot retains heat ferociously) but eat it anyway.

### Mother's Cooking — The Weekday Rotation

Japanese home cooking for families follows a predictable but beloved rotation:

1. **カレーライス (Curry Rice)**: Same as school curry but better — more meat, thicker, mom's secret addition (りんご honey, チョコレート, インスタントコーヒー — tiny amounts of each to deepen flavor). Made in a huge pot; lasts 2–3 days. Day 2 curry ("一晩寝かせたカレー" — curry that slept overnight) is widely considered superior to Day 1. Day 3 might become カレーうどん.

2. **ハンバーグ (Hamburg Steak)**: The #1 requested "mom's cooking" in Japanese family surveys. A thick, oval meat patty (beef+pork mix) pan-fried, then steamed with a lid, served with デミグラスソース (brown sauce), おろしポン酢 (grated daikon + ponzu), or 和風 (soy-based sauce). Carrot グラッセ (glazed carrots), broccoli, and ポテトサラダ are standard side dishes. The sizzle sound when poured sauce hits the hot hamburg is deeply satisfying.

3. **肉じゃが (Nikujaga)**: "Mom's Taste" (*おふくろの味*) incarnate. Thinly sliced beef, potatoes, carrots, onions, and しらたき (konnyaku noodles) simmered in soy sauce, sugar, mirin, and sake. Every mother makes it slightly differently. The potatoes absorb the broth and become translucent at the edges. Served in a small bowl with rice and miso soup.

4. **お好み焼き (Okonomiyaki)**: Cooked at the table on a ホットプレート (electric griddle). Kids participate in making their own — cracking eggs, mixing batter, flipping with two spatulas. The ソース (okonomiyaki sauce), マヨネーズ (Kewpie mayo), 青のり (aonori seaweed powder), and 鰹節 (bonito flakes dancing in the heat) topping ritual is performed with ceremony. In Nagoya, eating okonomiyaki with rice is normal (this confuses Osaka people).

5. **からあげ (Kara-age)**: Japanese fried chicken — soy-marinated, potato-starch-coated, double-fried. The smell of kara-age cooking signals a special dinner. Eaten with lemon and Kewpie mayo. The last piece is always a negotiation.

6. **ぎょうざ (Gyoza)**: Pan-fried pork dumplings. Often a collaborative family activity — mom makes the filling, kids wrap (badly, with too much filling, the first few burst during frying). Dipped in 酢醤油 (vinegar + soy sauce) with ラー油 (chili oil — optional for kids).

7. **うどん / そば**: Quick dinner. きつねうどん (udon with sweet fried tofu pouch), たぬきうどん (with tenkasu tempura scraps), 月見うどん (with raw egg on top). In Nagoya, 味噌煮込みうどん is the local pride.

8. **スパゲッティ**: Japanese-style pasta. ナポリタン (ketchup-based, with sausage and green pepper — a 喫茶店 classic), ミートソース, たらこスパゲッティ (cod roe + butter + nori). These are not Italian; they are their own category.

9. **チャーハン (Fried Rice)**: Quick weekday dinner using leftover rice. Ham, egg, green onion. Served with 中華スープ (Chinese-style egg soup) on the side.

10. **シチュー (Stew)**: Cream stew (ホワイトシチュー) with chicken, potatoes, carrots, broccoli, and mushrooms. Served with rice (not bread — the Japan rice-with-stew habit). Winter staple, alongside curry nights.

### お弁当 (Lunch Box) Culture — Field Trip Days

When there's a 遠足 (ensoku, field trip) or 運動会 (undōkai, sports day), kids bring お弁当 from home.

**Standard Kid Bento Contents:**
- たこさんウィンナー (Octopus-shaped sausages) — small red sausages with legs cut into them, which curl up when fried. Possibly the most recognizable symbol of Japanese childhood bento.
- 卵焼き (Tamagoyaki — rolled omelette) — slightly sweet or savory, cut into bite-sized pieces
- からあげ (Fried chicken) — 2–3 pieces
- ブロッコリー (Broccoli) — one small tree, often dressed with マヨ or sesame
- ミニトマト (Cherry tomatoes) — or, for tomato-hating kids, the silent negotiation of whether they can leave it uneaten
- おにぎり (Rice balls) — sometimes instead of rice in the bento box; wrapped in individual plastic wrap for freshness
- デザート (Dessert) — a small container of fruit (いちご, ぶどう, みかん) or a ゼリー cup
- The アルミカップ (aluminum foil cups) — small pleated cups separating each item

**Character Bento (キャラ弁 — Kyara-ben):**

A phenomenon since roughly 2005, where mothers shape the lunch into anime or game characters using nori seaweed, sliced cheese, colored rice (using でかふりかけ — deco furikake for rice coloring), and specialized tools (seaweed punches for eyes/mouths, egg molds, etc.).

*For a 1st–2nd grader:* The kid opens the bento box and squeals with delight. "ママみて！ピカチュウだ！" (Mom, look! It's Pikachu!). Classmates gather around. The kid is proud. They might eat it slowly, savoring the attention.

*For a 5th–6th grader:* The kid opens the box, sees a carefully crafted 鬼滅の刃 Tanjiro made of nori and egg. They close the lid quickly. "ママ…キャラ弁やめてって言ったじゃん…" (Mom... I told you to stop making character bento...). At this age, キャラ弁 becomes deeply embarrassing — it's "babyish." But some kids secretly appreciate it; they just can't admit it in front of friends.

The social transition from "キャラ弁最高！" to "キャラ弁やめて" happens around 4th grade, ±1 year, and it's a minor family drama that plays out in thousands of households.

## 4. Kids' Spending and Consumer Habits

### おこづかい (Allowance) by Grade

Rough national averages (2023 data):

| Grade | Monthly Allowance | Typical Purchase Power |
|-------|-------------------|----------------------|
| 1st (6–7) | ¥0–300 | 駄菓子 only; spending supervised |
| 2nd (7–8) | ¥300–500 | 駄菓子, cheap ガシャポン (¥100–200) |
| 3rd (8–9) | ¥500–800 | 駄菓子, ガシャポン ¥200–300, stationery |
| 4th (9–10) | ¥500–1,000 | All of above + Pokémon cards, small character goods |
| 5th (10–11) | ¥800–1,500 | Game ダウンロードカード (¥1,000–1,500), 駄菓子, 文房具, saving for larger items |
| 6th (11–12) | ¥1,000–2,000 | Larger purchases, ゲームソフト saving, お菓子 in larger quantities |

Note: These are *extremely* variable by family. Some families do no regular allowance; kids ask for money per purchase. Some do 年齢×100円 (age × 100 yen per month — a common rule of thumb). Some tie it to chores (お手伝い).

Nagoya families tend toward the higher end for dual-income households (both parents working at トヨタ関連企業 / Toyota-related companies, which dominate the Nagoya economy).

### お年玉 (New Year's Money)

A massive lump-sum injection of capital into the kid economy every January 1.

| Grade | Typical Total お年玉 | Sources |
|-------|----------------------|---------|
| 1st | ¥5,000–10,000 | Parents, grandparents × 2–4, occasional aunts/uncles |
| 3rd | ¥10,000–20,000 | |
| 6th | ¥20,000–40,000 | |

The first major consumer decision in a Japanese child's life: "お年玉でニンテンドースイッチを買う" (I'll buy a Nintendo Switch with my New Year's money). A Switch Lite is ~¥21,978; a Switch (OLED) is ~¥37,980. A sixth grader can theoretically afford one — and the negotiation with parents about whether they're *allowed* to spend it all at once is a major family drama.

### What Kids Save Up For

- **ゲームソフト (Game Software)**: ¥5,000–7,000 for a new release. ポケモン (Pokémon), スプラトゥーン (Splatoon), マリオカート (Mario Kart), あつまれどうぶつの森 (Animal Crossing: New Horizons), マインクラフト (Minecraft). The ダウンロード版 (download version) bought with a ニンテンドープリペイドカード (Nintendo Prepaid Card, ¥1,000 / ¥3,000 / ¥5,000 / ¥9,000) from the konbini is standard.
- **トレーディングカード (Trading Cards)**: ポケモンカード (Pokémon Cards) — ¥250–¥500 per booster pack of 5–10 cards. Kids buy with allowance or nag parents at the konbini counter. Also デュエル・マスターズ (Duel Masters), 遊戯王 (Yu-Gi-Oh!), ワンピースカードゲーム (One Piece Card Game).
- **ガシャポン (Capsule Toy Machines)**: ¥100–500 per play. Found outside スーパー (supermarkets), 家電量販店 (electronics stores like ビックカメラ/エディオン), 駄菓子屋, game centers. Current popular series: ポケモン, 鬼滅の刃, すみっコぐらし, ちいかわ (Chiikawa — the current dominant cute character). The ¥300 and ¥400 price points are increasingly common; the ¥100ガシャポン is becoming rare. A kid with ¥500 will check 3 machines, evaluate the ラインナップ (lineup) carefully, and make a calculated choice. The thrill of turning the handle (ガチャっと) and the capsule clunking down the chute is addictive.
- **文房具 (Stationery)**: See Section 7.
- **駄菓子**: Daily small purchases; the ¥30–100 spending range.

### 駄菓子屋 (Traditional Candy Shops)

A declining but culturally vital institution. These are tiny, often family-run shops, packed floor to ceiling with glass jars and cardboard boxes of cheap candy and toys. The owner is often an elderly person who knows all the neighborhood kids by name.

**The 駄菓子屋 Experience:**
- Enter through a narrow doorway, a bell jingles
- Wooden floor, slightly sticky
- Everything is 10–50 yen per piece
- The くじ引き (lottery draw) — a cardboard box with strings hanging out; ¥10–50 per pull. Prizes range from a piece of gum to a スーパーボール (super ball) to a small toy. Often rigged to let kids "win" occasionally.
- The メンコ (menko — card-flipping game cards) and ビー玉 (marbles) section
- Small plastic 模型 (model kits) for ¥50–100 — a tiny glider, a small car
- The shopkeeper who remembers which candy you like

In Nagoya, 駄菓子屋 are fewer than in Tokyo but still present in older neighborhoods (大須 Ōsu shopping district, parts of 中村区, 昭和区). A typical scene: four kids on bikes pull up to 藤田駄菓子屋 (Fujita Dagashiya), each with ¥150 in coins. Twenty minutes of deliberation. They leave with paper bags rustling with うまい棒, ラムネ, ココアシガレット, and one shared くじ引き that was a dud.

The decline of 駄菓子屋 is a real melancholy for Japanese culture — replaced, functionally, by the konbini and Amazon. A kid in a mystery story who frequents a 駄菓子屋 signals that their neighborhood still has the "old Japan" texture.

### The Nagoya Economy Note

Nagoya has the highest rate of dual-income families and the highest rate of car ownership among Japan's major cities (the city was largely rebuilt after WWII bombing with wide roads, unlike Tokyo's narrow streets). This means:
- Kids are more likely to be driven places, but also more likely to have their own house key (カギっ子, *kagikko* — latchkey kid) for after-school
- Disposable income for kid items is relatively high
- Toyota/Lexus culture means some kids' families have company-provided housing and stable incomes

## 5. 通学 (Commuting to School)

### 集団登校 (Group Commute)

In most Japanese elementary schools, especially for grades 1–3, kids walk to school in a group (集団登校, *shūdan tōkō*) organized by neighborhood block (町内会 or 通学班). A 班長 (hanchō, group leader) — usually a 5th or 6th grader — leads the group.

**The Morning Routine:**
1. 7:30 AM: Kids gather at the neighborhood 集合場所 (meeting spot) — typically a park, a street corner with a 通学路 sign, or outside the 公民館 (community center).
2. The 班長 takes attendance: はい、全員います (Everyone's here).
3. The group walks in a column of twos, youngest toward the front.
4. 旗当番 (hata tōban) — a parent volunteer wearing a yellow vest and holding a yellow flag stands at dangerous intersections to stop cars while the group crosses. Every parent in the neighborhood takes turns.
5. Greeting ritual: every child calls out 「おはようございます！」(Good morning!) to every adult they pass — including 旗当番 parents, elderly neighbors sweeping the street, the 交番の巡査 (police box officer), and crossing guards.
6. 8:00–8:15 AM: Arrive at school.

**The Afternoon Return:**
- Grades 1–2 typically leave school by 2:00 PM (5th period max), sometimes with teacher escort
- Grades 3–4 by 3:00 PM
- Grades 5–6 by 3:45 PM (6th period)
- Lower grades walk home in the same group; upper grades often walk home freely with friends

This daily walk is a major source of observation and memory — the changing seasons, the neighborhood dogs, which houses have こども110番 stickers, which コンビニ is on the route, the 自動販売機 (vending machine) that glows invitingly.

### ランドセル (Randoseru) — Weight Issues

The ランドセル is a hard leather backpack that every Japanese elementary schooler carries. It's a major family purchase — a good one costs ¥30,000–80,000, and grandparents often gift it for school entry (入学祝い).

**The Problem:** By mid-elementary, the randoseru becomes absurdly heavy.
- Textbooks (国語, 算数, 理科, 社会, 英語 — starting from 3rd grade, plus 道徳, 図工, 家庭科, 保健)
- Notebooks (multiple)
- 筆箱 (pencil case)
- 連絡帳 (communication notebook)
- 給食着 (lunch duty apron + cap + mask — on duty weeks)
- 体操着 (PE uniform) and 上履き (indoor shoes) in 手提げ袋 (tote bag)
- 水筒 (water bottle — now required year-round due to heat)
- タブレット (tablet) — many schools issue iPads or Chromebooks for home; some kids carry these daily

Total weight for a 5th grader: **5–8 kg** on a body weighing 30–35 kg. That's roughly 15–25% of body weight. Orthopedic concerns have been raised nationally. Some schools now allow kids to leave textbooks at school (置き勉 — *okiben*, "leave-study"), but the practice isn't universal.

A kid walking home with a heavy randoseru, slumping forward slightly, is an iconic Japanese visual.

**Randoseru Culture:**
- Colors: 1st graders always get 赤 (red) for girls, 黒 (black) for boys — this is so universal it feels like natural law, though it's just custom. By 2015s, some parents choose ピンク, 茶色, 紺, 水色 — girls might resist if not red. In 2020s, the color barrier has relaxed significantly: 紫, 緑, オレンジ.
- The ランドセルカバー (randoseru cover): A transparent or patterned plastic cover. The 黄色いカバー (yellow cover) is distributed to all 1st graders by the police/school — it makes them visible to cars. By 2nd grade, kids can take it off or switch to a character cover.
- The 防犯ブザー (security buzzer): A small electronic buzzer clipped to the randoseru shoulder strap. Pull the cord, it emits an ear-splitting alarm. Kids are taught to pull it if a stranger approaches. Schools hold annual 防犯訓練 (security drills). The buzzer's battery must be checked periodically.
- The ランドセルの思い出 (Randoseru Memories): After six years, the randoseru carries marks — scuffs from falling, sticker residue from a brief sticker-collecting phase, fading on the shoulder strap, a small tear from being dropped off a playground slide. Some families keep the randoseru as a keepsake; some have it remade into a wallet or key case as a 卒業記念 (graduation memento).

### Walking Routes, Landmarks Kids Notice

On the daily walk to and from school, kids notice specific things that adults tune out:

- **自動販売機 (Vending Machines)**: Every kid knows the exact location of every 自販機 on the route. The one that sells ココア (hot in winter, sells out fast), the one that sometimes gives two drinks by accident (mythical, but "マジで出た！" stories circulate), the one with the おしるこ (sweet red bean soup) can that nobody buys.
- **犬 (Dogs)**: "あの家の柴犬、触らせてくれる" (That house's Shiba Inu lets you pet it), "あっちの道のチワワ、めっちゃ吠えるから行かない" (The Chihuahua on that street barks like crazy, we don't go that way). Kids develop complex mental maps of which houses have friendly or scary dogs.
- **工事現場 (Construction Sites)**: Endlessly fascinating to younger boys. "ここ、前は空き地だったのにマンション建ってる" (This used to be an empty lot, now they're building an apartment).
- **季節の変化 (Seasonal Changes)**: The 桜 (cherry blossoms) on the 通学路 in April, the アジサイ (hydrangeas) in June, the 金木犀 (kinmokusei — fragrant olive) smell in October, the 落ち葉 (fallen leaves) that are fun to kick, the 雪 (rare in Nagoya, but when it happens, it's a major event).
- **コンビニ**: The 7-Eleven at the big intersection, the Lawson at the station, the FamilyMart near the park. (See Section 1.)
- **公園 (Parks)**: Every route has at least one. The swings, the slide, the ジャングルジム (jungle gym). Where kids detour to play on the way home.

### Rain Gear

On rainy days, the morning scene transforms:
- **傘 (Kasa — Umbrella)**: Every kid has their own umbrella. Character umbrellas are standard for lower grades (ポケモン, すみっコ, 鬼滅の刃). By upper grades, plain colored folding umbrellas become cooler. The 透明ビニール傘 (clear vinyl umbrella) — ¥500 at any konbini — is the universal backup.
- **長靴 (Nagagutsu — Rain boots)**: Brightly colored for younger kids; plain black or navy for older. Brand: ムーンスター (MoonStar) or アサヒ (Asahi) are common.
- **レインコート (Raincoat)**: For lower grades, often a full-body yellow raincoat with randoseru cover — the kid walks inside a nylon tent.
- **ランドセルカバー (Randoseru Rain Cover)**: A waterproof pull-over cover that protects the backpack. Bright yellow or transparent.
- **水筒 (Water Bottle)**: Still required even in rain; never skipped.
- The classroom 傘立て (umbrella stand) outside each classroom door — rows of dripping umbrellas, occasional disputes about "that's MY umbrella."

### 黄色い帽子 (Yellow Hat) — First Graders

All 1st graders wear a bright yellow hat (黄色い帽子) for the entire first year. This is nearly universal across Japan. The purpose:
1. Makes them visible to drivers (the yellow pops against the urban background)
2. Identifies them as a 1st grader to the community (strangers know to watch out for them; older kids know to be patient)
3. The hat becomes a symbol of early childhood — taking it off after graduating 1st grade is a rite of passage

The hat is typically a baseball cap or bucket hat style, made of lightweight cotton, with the school emblem printed or sewn on the front. By May or June, many have small stains or pen marks.

*Narrative Potential:* A lost yellow hat — finding it, who returned it, where it was found — is a classic children's mystery setup.

### 通学路 Safety: こども110番の家 (Children's 110 Emergency Houses)

A nationwide program started after the 2001 Ikeda school massacre and reinforced after subsequent child abductions. Local households and businesses volunteer to be こども110番の家 (Child Emergency Number 110 Houses):
- They display a distinctive sticker with the 110番 logo and a cartoon police officer on their front gate or door
- Kids are taught: "If a stranger follows you, or you feel unsafe for any reason, run to the nearest こども110番の家 and shout たすけて！(Help!)"
- The resident calls 110 and shelters the child until police arrive
- On the walk to school, kids mentally note which houses are 110番の家 — this becomes part of the route's mental map

Komeda Coffee (コメダ珈琲店) — the Nagoya-based chain — participates in this program at many of its locations. "コメダに逃げ込め" (run into Komeda) is actual advice some Nagoya kids receive. The chain's signature red-brick exterior and comfortable booth seating make it psychologically a "safe" place.

## 6. After-School Life

### 学童保育 (Gakudō Hoiku — After-School Care)

With Nagoya's high rate of dual-income families, 学童保育 (also called 放課後児童クラブ — after-school children's club) is a major institution. Kids whose parents work until 6–7 PM go to 学童 instead of going home.

**Typical Gakudō Experience:**
- Walk from school to the 学童 facility (sometimes in the same school building, sometimes a separate community center)
- Sign in: ただいま！(I'm home!) 
- Snack time (おやつ) provided
- Do homework (宿題)
- Free play: 折り紙 (origami), トランプ (playing cards), UNO, 人生ゲーム (Game of Life board game), けん玉 (kendama), コマ (spinning tops), 読書 (reading)
- Outdoor play in the attached playground if weather permits
- Pickup by parent (often dad, in Nagoya, since Toyota-related companies tend to have reasonable overtime policies) between 5:30 and 7:00 PM
- The last kids waiting at 7 PM watch the door with quiet desperation — a well-known emotional beat

Gakudō Kid vs. Home Kid is a minor social division. Gakudō kids tend to be closer friends with other gakudō kids.

### 習い事 (Lessons / Extracurriculars)

Nearly every Japanese elementary schooler does at least one 習い事. The classic roster:

| Lesson | Age Start | Monthly Cost | Frequency | Gender Split |
|--------|-----------|--------------|-----------|-------------|
| **スイミング (Swimming)** | 3–5 | ¥7,000–10,000 | 1–2×/week | Equal |
| **ピアノ (Piano)** | 4–6 | ¥6,000–10,000 | 1×/week | ~70% girls, 30% boys |
| **英会話 (English Conversation)** | 5–7 | ¥8,000–15,000 | 1×/week | Equal |
| **そろばん (Abacus)** | 6–8 | ¥4,000–7,000 | 2×/week | Equal |
| **習字 (Calligraphy)** | 6–8 | ¥3,000–6,000 | 1×/week | Equal, skews girl |
| **サッカー (Soccer)** | 5–7 | ¥5,000–8,000 | 1–2×/week | ~90% boys |
| **野球 (Baseball)** | 6–8 | ¥5,000–10,000 | 2–3×/week | ~95% boys, very intense |
| **ダンス (Dance)** | 5–7 | ¥5,000–8,000 | 1×/week | ~85% girls |
| **バレエ (Ballet)** | 4–6 | ¥8,000–12,000 | 1–2×/week | ~99% girls |
| **空手 / 柔道 (Karate / Judo)** | 6–8 | ¥5,000–8,000 | 1–2×/week | ~70% boys |
| **プログラミング (Programming)** | 7–10 | ¥8,000–15,000 | 1×/week | ~80% boys |

In Nagoya specifically:
- **スイミング** is the #1 extracurricular, driven by the summer heat and the desire for basic safety. Nagoya summers are brutal (35°C+ with extreme humidity).
- **英会話** is very popular among Toyota-affiliated families who anticipate international postings or who work with English-speaking colleagues.
- **そろばん** has an unusually strong presence in Aichi — the 全国そろばん競技大会 (National Abacus Competition) has historically had strong Aichi representatives.

The over-scheduled kid (月・水泳、火・ピアノ、木・英会話、金・そろばん = Monday swimming, Tuesday piano, Thursday English, Friday abacus) is a recognizable type — tired, carrying multiple bags, doing homework in the car between lessons.

### Free Play: 公園 (Park) and 友達の家 (Friend's House)

**公園 Culture:**
- The neighborhood park is the default destination. Equipment: ブランコ (swings — two seats, always contested), 滑り台 (slide), ジャングルジム (jungle gym), 砂場 (sandbox), 鉄棒 (horizontal bar).
- The ブランコ立ち乗り (standing on the swing) is technically forbidden but universally attempted by 3rd–4th grade boys.
- 砂場 with its population of abandoned toys (broken plastic shovels, rusted trucks) and the mysterious cat that sometimes uses it as a litter box.
- 鬼ごっこ (Onigokko — tag), かくれんぼ (Kakurenbo — hide and seek), ドッジボール (dodgeball).
- 虫取り (Mushitori — bug catching) in summer: セミ (cicadas), カブトムシ (rhinoceros beetles), クワガタ (stag beetles). A kid with a カブトムシ in a small plastic case is a temporary celebrity.
- ダンゴムシ (pill bugs/roly-polies) — the universal starter bug for small children. Every Japanese kid has spent time poking pill bugs until they roll into balls.

**友達の家 (Friend's House):**
- The formal ritual: "お邪魔します" (Excuse me for intruding) at the genkan (entryway), shoes off, placed neatly side by side.
- The friend's mom offers おやつ and ジュース (juice) — this is expected. "おかあさん、おやつ食べていい？" (Mom, can [friend] have snacks? ").
- The friend's room — usually piled with Pokémon cards, Nintendo Switch dock, manga volumes (コロコロコミック for boys, ちゃお or りぼん for girls), and a 勉強机 (study desk) that is more storage surface than study surface.
- The unspoken hierarchy of houses: whose mom makes the best snacks, whose house has the biggest TV, whose house has the most games, whose house you're NOT allowed to visit ("あそこの家、お母さんがうるさいから行かない" — "We don't go to that house, their mom is strict").

### Video Games

**Platform:** Nintendo Switch dominates elementary-age gaming. The Switch Lite (¥21,978) is the affordable entry point; the Switch OLED (¥37,980) is the premium one.

**Key Games for Elementary Kids (2024 landscape):**

| Game | Nickname | Player Base | Parents' View |
|------|----------|------------|---------------|
| **ポケットモンスター スカーレット/バイオレット** | ポケモンSV | Universal, all grades | Acceptable; nostalgic for parents who played Red/Green |
| **あつまれ どうぶつの森** | あつ森 | Girls especially, but universal during COVID boom | Approved; "creative" and "calming" |
| **スプラトゥーン3** | スプラ3 | Upper elementary (4th+), competitive boys | Slightly concerning (shooter, but ink-not-blood) |
| **マインクラフト** | マイクラ | Universal, lean male | Approved; "educational" — building, logic, cooperation |
| **マリオカート8 デラックス** | マリカ | Universal, all ages | Approved; family-friendly |
| **大乱闘スマッシュブラザーズ SPECIAL** | スマブラ | Boys, group play at friends' houses | Approved for parties |
| **フォートナイト** | フォトナ | Upper elementary (4th+), boys | The problematic one — online voice chat with strangers, microtransactions, addictive. Source of countless parent-child conflicts. |
| **スイカゲーム** | スイカ | Universal (cheap, 240 yen download) | Approved; "it's just fruit" |

**Gaming Culture:**
- 通信プレイ (Online Multiplayer): Kids arrange to meet online after school — "今日何時からスプラやる？" (What time today should we play Splatoon?). This replaces the 公園 meetup for many.
- フレンドコード (Friend Codes): Trading these at school is a social activity.
- Parental controls: みまもり設定 (Nintendo's parental monitoring app) lets parents set daily time limits (typical: 30 minutes to 1 hour on school days, 1–2 hours on weekends). The "あと5分です" (5 minutes remaining) warning that pops up mid-game is a universal source of childhood stress.
- イオン / ゲオ (Aeon / Geo) game sections: These stores have Nintendo Switch demo stations. On weekends, a cluster of kids huddled around the Switch demo, playing Mario Kart, is a common sight.

### YouTube / Online Entertainment

Hiragana search terms typed clumsily into the YouTube search bar:

**What Kids Watch:**
- **Hikakin / Hajime Syacho** — the two kings of Japanese kid-friendly YouTube. Hikakin (ヒカキン) does game play, product reviews, and challenges; Hajime Syacho (はじめしゃちょー) does elaborate, often absurd stunts. Both are household names among Japanese elementary schoolers.
- **Fischer's (フィッシャーズ)** — group channel doing challenges, pranks, hide-and-seek in big locations
- **ポケモン Kids TV** — official Pokémon kids channel
- **すしらーめん《りく》** — elaborate Rube Goldberg / domino setups; boys especially
- **ゲーム実況 (Let's Plays)** — kids watch other people play Minecraft (*マイクラ実況*), Splatoon, horror games
- **手抜き料理 / 大食い (Cooking Shortcuts / Mukbang)** — oddly popular with kids; watching someone eat 5 kg of ramen
- **Shorts (YouTube Shorts)**: Infinite scroll of short clips; the algorithmic drain on attention is real

**Device Situation:**
- Most elementary kids don't have their own smartphone until 5th–6th grade (and even then, it's a キッズケータイ — kids' phone with limited functions)
- Many use the family iPad or their mom's old smartphone on home WiFi
- ゲーム機でYouTube (YouTube on the Nintendo Switch) is common — the Switch has a YouTube app
- キッズYouTube (YouTube Kids app) for younger kids — theoretically filtered, practically still full of weird content

## 7. Stationery and School Supplies (文房具 and 学校用品)

### 筆箱 (Fudebako — Pencil Case)

A massive status item. Two main types:

**布製 (Fabric/Case Type):**
- A rectangular box-shaped case that zips open, revealing organized compartments
- Characters: ポケモン, すみっコぐらし, 鬼滅の刃, ちいかわ, ドラえもん
- A ¥1,000–2,000 character 筆箱 is standard. Design changes each school year.
- By 5th grade, many kids switch to plain colors (black, navy, pastel) — character designs "卒業" (graduation from character goods) is a recognized social transition

**缶ペンケース (Tin/Can Type):**
- The double-decker metal case with magnets holding it closed
- Opens by pressing a button or sliding a catch; the two sides fan out
- Built-in 鉛筆削り (pencil sharpener) on the inside
- Pro: holds a lot, satisfying magnetic *clack*
- Con: LOUD. Drop it in class during a quiet moment and everyone turns around. The dropped-缶ペンケース moment is a universal classroom experience.
- Filled with: 鉛筆 5–6 (sharpened, arranged by length for aesthetic satisfaction), 赤鉛筆 (red pencil for grading/underlining), 消しゴム, 定規 (ruler, 15cm), 名前ペン (name pen — permanent marker for labeling belongings)

**Inside the Pencil Case — What's Cool:**
- **シャーペン (Mechanical Pencil)**: Officially "not allowed until junior high" in many schools, but kids start sneaking them in during 4th–5th grade. The クルトガ (Kurutoga — the one where the lead rotates to stay sharp) by 三菱鉛筆 is the holy grail. The ドクターグリップ (Dr. Grip) by Pilot is also cool.
- **ふではこ (Brush Pen Case)**: Required for 習字 (calligraphy class). Separate from the regular 筆箱.

### 鉛筆 (Pencils)

Kids use **2B** pencils in early elementary (thicker, softer, darker — easier for small hands). By 3rd–4th grade, they transition to **B**. Adults use HB.

| Brand | Significance |
|-------|-------------|
| **トンボ鉛筆 (Tombow)** | The classic yellow-and-black pencil. The 8900 model is the standard. |
| **三菱鉛筆 (Mitsubishi Pencil / uni)** | The premium brand. ユニ (uni) and ハイユニ (Hi-uni) pencils have a cult following. Hi-uni is the "rich kid" pencil. |
| **名入れ鉛筆 (Name-engraved Pencils)** | A common gift for school entry — pencils with the child's name printed in gold or silver. The kid's full name in hiragana, in gold, on a red pencil. |

Pencil maintenance: Sharpening is a meditative practice. The classroom 鉛筆削り (electric or manual sharpener mounted on a shelf) collects shavings that a 日直 (daily duty student) empties into the burnable trash. The satisfying *grrrr* of the sharpener, the smell of cedar and graphite.

### 消しゴム (Erasers)

| Brand | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| **MONO消しゴム (Tombow)** | The classic — white rectangular block with the blue, black, and white diagonal stripe paper sleeve. Arguably the most recognizable eraser in Japan. |
| **カドケシ (Kadokeshi)** | Instead of corners, it has many edges — the idea being you always have a sharp corner. 28 corners. The kid who has this is either practical or just likes the gimmick. |
| **香り付き消しゴム (Scented Erasers)** | Fruit-scented: いちご, レモン, ぶどう, メロン, コーラ. Popular with girls. The scent fades after a week. Collectible. |
| **キャラクター消しゴム** | Shaped like Pokémon, animals, food. Terrible at actually erasing. For display, not use. |

**消しゴムバトル (Eraser Battles):**
A classic classroom game: each kid places their eraser on the desk surface. Flick it with a finger toward the opponent's eraser. If you knock theirs off the desk edge (or touch theirs with yours), you win. Teachers usually ban this, but it's played anyway when their back is turned. The MONO eraser's rectangular shape makes it the preferred weapon — stable, flickable, aerodynamically adequate.

### 定規 (Ruler), コンパス (Compass), 分度器 (Protractor)

- **定規 (Jōgi — Ruler)**: 15 cm standard. Transparent plastic with a subtle blue tint and printed centimeter marks that eventually wear off. Character-design rulers exist (the inside has an anime image you can see through the plastic) but are considered ダサい (uncool) by upper elementary.
- **三角定規 (Sankaku Jōgi — Triangle Rulers)**: Two needed — one with 30°/60°/90° angles, one with 45°/45°/90°. Used for 算数 (math) starting in 3rd grade. Stored in a plastic case with the 分度器.
- **コンパス (Konpasu — Compass)**: The classic silver metal compass with a pencil lead holder and a small wheel at the top for turning. Pin-sharp metal point that leaves marks on desks. The safety compass (plastic, blunt needle) is considered babyish by older kids despite being safer.
- **分度器 (Bundoki — Protractor)**: Semi-circular, 180°, transparent plastic. The 360° full-circle protractor exists but the 180° half-circle is standard.

### 下敷き (Shitajiki — Writing Board)

A thin, flexible plastic sheet slipped under the paper to provide a smooth writing surface (since Japanese school desks are wooden and slightly textured). Two sides:
- One side: plain, for all-purpose use
- Other side: printed with a multiplication table (九九表), a map of Japan, the periodic table, or anime characters

Character 下敷き is a massive category: 鬼滅の刃, 呪術廻戦, ポケモン, スプラトゥーン, ちいかわ, すみっコぐらし, ドラえもん, 名探偵コナン. A kid might switch 下敷き every semester to follow the current cool character. Sticker-decorated 下敷き (covering every square millimeter with stickers, then layering tape over it) is a 4th–5th grade girl phenomenon.

### 連絡帳 (Renrakuchō — Communication Notebook)

A small, stapled, soft-cover notebook with pre-printed columns. Functions as a daily bridge between school and home:

**Each Day's Page:**
1. 日付 (Date)
2. 明日の時間割 (Tomorrow's Schedule) — written by the kid, from the board
3. 持ち物 (Things to Bring) — listed items for each subject
4. 宿題 (Homework) — assignments due tomorrow
5. 先生からの連絡 (From Teacher): announcements, reminders, sometimes a brief note about behavior
6. 家からの連絡 (From Home): parent's signature or 印鑑 (seal/stamp) confirming they checked the notebook. A missing parent's sign results in the teacher asking "連絡帳、お家の人に見せた？" (Did you show the renrakuchō to your family?). Also: notes about 早退 (early dismissal), 欠席連絡 (absence notification), doctor's appointment.

The 保護者印 (parent's seal stamp) — a small red 印鑑 that parents stamp in the 連絡帳 daily — is a subtle class marker. A cheap 100-yen stamp, an elegant ivory 印鑑 in a paulownia box, a pre-inked Shachihata stamp: each tells a story.

Kids are supposed to show the 連絡帳 to parents, but many forget, leading to last-minute 7 AM panic: "今日、絵の具いるんだった！" (I need paint supplies today!). The 連絡帳 is the nexus of Japanese elementary school's intense parent-school communication loop.

### Other Essential School Supplies

- **手提げ袋 (Tesage-bukuro — Tote Bag)**: For carrying 体操着 (PE uniform) and 上履き (indoor shoes). Fabric, drawstring or open-top, with the kid's name written large in permanent marker. P.E. day is announced in advance; forgetting it is a minor social disaster (the kid has to call home or borrow from the nurse's office).
- **上履き (Uwabaki — Indoor Shoes)**: White canvas slip-ons with a colored elastic band across the top. The color of the elastic band indicates grade (red = 1st, blue = 2nd, etc. — varies by school). Kids write their name in big kanji/hiragana on the heel so they can find their shoes among the identical rows. The 下駄箱 (getabako — shoe locker) at the school entrance has a cubby for each student.
- **絵の具セット (Paint Set)**: A green or red plastic case containing watercolor paints, brushes, a palette, a small water bucket, and a rag. Smells of dried paint. The kid whose set is pristine doesn't actually use it; the kid whose set is a mess of mixed colors and bent brush tips is the one who loves art.
- **習字セット (Calligraphy Set)**: Required for 書道 (shodō). A rectangular box containing: 墨汁 (liquid sumi ink in a plastic bottle — traditional ink sticks are used in clubs but not in class), 硯 (suzuri — ink stone), 筆 (brush), 文鎮 (bunchin — paperweight), 下敷き (calligraphy-specific felt mat). The smell of sumi ink is a quintessential Japanese classroom memory. Spilling the ink is a disaster that stains everything.
- **鍵盤ハーモニカ (Kenban Harmonica — Melodica)**: A keyboard instrument you blow into through a tube. Every kid has one. Pink for girls, blue for boys (stereotypically; many schools have moved to neutral). The sound of a 2nd grade music class — 30 melodicas playing かえるの合唱 (Frog Chorus) simultaneously — is unforgettable and mythic.
- **リコーダー (Recorder)**: Soprano recorder, starting from 3rd grade. The plastic Yamaha or Aulos recorder. "ソプラノリコーダー" is the formal name. The squeak of an incorrectly fingered note haunts Japanese elementary school hallways.

## 8. Seasonal Consumer Items

### 冬 (Winter — December through February)

**カイロ (Kairo — Hand Warmers / Body Warmers):**
- Small packets containing iron powder, salt, water, and vermiculite. When exposed to air, they oxidize and generate heat (50–60°C) for 10–20 hours. Pure chemistry, pure comfort.
- **貼るカイロ (Haru Kairo — Stick-on type)**: Adhesive backing. Stuck directly to undershirt on the lower back or stomach. The #1 winter item for kids walking to school. Myojo (ミョウジョウ) and Kiribai (桐灰) are the main brands.
- **ミニカイロ (Mini Kairo)**: Hand-sized, non-adhesive. Held in pockets or gloves. Often given out for free at store entrances as promotional items.
- Price: ¥30–60 per individual packet; a box of 30 for ¥500–800 at drugstores.
- The morning ritual: mom opens a カイロ packet and sticks one on each kid's back before they put on their undershirt. The slow spread of warmth on the walk to school.

**Snow-Related Items (Nagoya-Specific):**
- Nagoya gets very little snow (1–2 light dustings per year). When it snows even slightly, it's a citywide event. Schools might delay start, kids go wild.
- Snow gear is thus minimal: most kids don't own snow boots. They wear normal 長靴.
- 手袋 (Tebukuro — Gloves): Wool or acrylic, often connected by a string threaded through coat sleeves so they don't get lost. Lost single gloves are a common 落とし物 (lost item). Character gloves for younger kids; plain dark gloves by upper elementary.

### 夏 (Summer — June through August)

Nagoya summers are famously brutal — consistently among the hottest in Japan, with temperatures reaching 37°C (99°F) and humidity above 80%. The city sits in a basin (濃尾平野 / Nōbi Plain) that traps heat.

**虫除けスプレー (Mushi-yoke Spray — Bug Repellent):**
- Applied before going to the park or on summer evenings
- Common brands: **スキンベープ (Skin Vape)** by Fumakilla, **サラテクト (Sara-tekuto)** by Earth Chemical
- The pink スキンベープ bottle is iconic
- Sprayed on arms, legs, and neck from about 20 cm away; the chemical-sweet smell of DEET or picaridin mingles with sunscreen
- ¥500–800 per bottle

**虫刺され (Mushi-sasare — Bug Bite Treatment):**
- The aftermath of summer play. Mosquito bites (蚊に刺された — literally "bitten by mosquito") swell into red bumps
- Treatment: **ムヒ (Muhi)** — a liquid antihistamine in a distinctive cream-colored bottle with green/blue text. Applied with a rollerball or sponge tip. The menthol cooling sensation followed by itch relief. ¥500–700.
- **ウナコーワ (Una Kowa)** — competitor to Muhi; liquid also, slightly different formulation
- **かゆみ止めパッチ (Itch-relief patches)** — small adhesive patches infused with antihistamine, stuck directly on bites. The kid walks around dotted with them.
- **蚊取り線香 (Katori Senkō — Mosquito Coils)**: The green spiral incense coil, burned in a ceramic pig-shaped holder (蚊遣り豚 / Kayari-buta). The smell of summer evening. Parents light them on the balcony or in the garden, not typically taken to school.

**日焼け止め (Hiyakedome — Sunscreen):**
- Applied by mom before school on hot days
- **ビオレ (Biore)**, **アネッサ (Anessa)** by Shiseido — Anessa is the premium brand
- ¥800–2,000
- The morning battle: kids hate the greasy feeling and try to escape application. "動かないで！首、まだ塗ってない！" (Stay still! I haven't done your neck yet!)
- The barcode-stripe of missed sunscreen on the side of the neck, revealed by a distinctive sunburn stripe

**水着 (Mizugi — Swimsuit):**
- Pool season runs June through late July (or early September for some schools)
- School swimsuit: standardized by school. Boys: dark blue or black shorts-style. Girls: dark blue one-piece with a name tag sewn on the chest. The school-issued swimsuit is kept in a mesh bag with the kid's name.
- ラッシュガード (Rash Guard): Increasingly allowed at schools for sun protection, though some traditional schools still ban them (skin exposure = character building is the old-school logic). A point of parent-school friction.
- Private swimsuits for summer vacation: colorful, with character prints for younger kids. Girls' swimsuits increasingly incorporate rash guards and shorts.

**扇子 (Sensu) and うちわ (Uchiwa — Folding and Flat Fans):**
- Most kids have a small folding fan (扇子) in their randoseru during summer. Cheap ones (¥100–300 at 百均 / 100-yen shop) with plastic ribs and patterned paper.
- うちわ (flat round fan): Given out free at festivals, stores, and as promotional items. Often printed with advertisements. A drawer at home has a stack of うちわ collected over years.
- The classroom fans: ceiling fans spinning at max speed, windows wide open, curtains billowing. Kids fanning themselves with 下敷き during tests. The teacher's desk fan — a small clip-on USB fan, slightly unfair.
- **冷却タオル (Reikyaku Taoru — Cooling Towels)**: Fabric that stays cool when wet. Worn around the neck during outdoor activities. ¥300–500 at 百均.
- **ネッククーラー (Neck Cooler)**: The 2020s evolution — a battery-powered neck fan (worn like headphones around the neck, blowing air upward). ¥2,000–5,000 at electronics stores. Increasingly common among kids, though some schools restrict their use.

### 夏休み (Summer Vacation) — Late July through August

- 40 days of freedom with a 夏休みの友 (summer vacation workbook) to complete
- ラジオ体操 (Radio Calisthenics): Kids gather at the neighborhood park at 6:30 AM, do group exercises to NHK radio, get a stamp on a card. Attendance = proof of a structured morning. Older kids grudgingly attend; younger kids bring their stamp card proudly.
- 自由研究 (Independent Research Project): Every kid must complete one. Themes range from "observing morning glories" (アサガオ観察 — the standard 1st grader project) to elaborate dioramas or science experiments that are clearly 80% parent-made.
- プール開放 (Pool Open Days): The school pool is open select days, supervised by teachers and parent volunteers. Kids come just to play — no lessons, just splashing.
